General Advice

The only part of the student loan you have control over are the additional repayments. Additional repayments are voluntary repayments you make on top of your mandatory repayments.

Making additional repayments is how you can save money by paying off your loans quicker.

If the amount you save by making additional repayments is positive then you will save money. If it is negative, then you are losing money by making additional repayments.

You should use the APR to assess how much you are saving as a proportion of what you have spent. You can also compare the APR to current bank rates, the higher the better.

Keep in mind that the total amount you spend on additional repayments can be put in a savings account or in other investments which may give a better return. See here for other options.

Additional repayment guidance

Below are three charts that will help you decide how much you should repay additionally.

Additional repayments are repayments you make on top of what you must repay each month. Visit the Student Loans Company website to see how to make additional repayments.

Making additional repayments can save you money. However, it is possible to lose money by making these repayments. We highly recommend you look at these charts before you make additional repayments!

You may be in a position where you save only a small amount compared to how much you put in. Therefore, if you have a low APR, we recommend you take a look at our other suggested methods of saving/investing.

Chart One - Description

This chart shows what making additional repayments does to:

Note that For small loans and/or large repayments, all three charts start to look strange as the estimations are more uncertain. APR will become unusable and care should be taken when interpreting.

Chart Two

Chart Two - Description

This is the most important chart! It gives you an estimated APR for your additional repayments.

Here's why it's important; although you might save some money by making additional repayments, you might have spent a lot in the process.

Think of it this way, the higher the APR, the more worth it is to make additional repayments.

Before you make additional repayments, you should compare the APR to savings account interest rates. It may be worth putting your money into other savings schemes.

Assumptions you should be aware of:
  • We assumed a fixed income, inflation and repayment thresholds until your loan is cancelled or you pay it off. In reality, these will increase or decrease through the duration of your loan.

  • We assumed fixed interest rates until your loan is cancelled or you pay it off.

  • We assume you stay in the same country.

  • We assume a fixed currency exchange rate as specified by the Student Loans Company.

  • We assume any one time repayments are made immediately.

  • We assume benefits from making additional repayments are felt immediately after paying the loan off. In reality you will feel the benefits both before and after the loan is paid off.
